I've never really understood how freezing a live moment on paper or screen can give that moment more life, make it appear more vibrant and more meaningful than when you were actually living it. I don't know whether it's the experience of reliving a moment from the past through a slightly different version of ourselves, the ability to examine some details we may have missed, or something more. I suspect it's something more, having to do with secrets buried in the mechanisms of our cognition. What I do know is how images make me feel, and I suppose that's my attraction to photography.

I don't have a dark room any more, and can't justify building one. Still, I sometimes feel that the instantaneous gratification afforded by digital photography, and its magnification of my skills, whatever they are, make the art feel less personal to me. I sense the images are technically more beautiful but with less of my own touch in them, for better or worse. I certainly wouldn't intentionally degrade the technical quality of an image, but I always try to keep the technology from washing out the human imperfections of my perspective. We're imperfect and we perceive imperfectly, and that is important to remember, and more important yet to preserve. Photography, for me, represents the balance between the love I have for technology and the struggle to keep it from completely scrubbing away our perfect human imperfections.

I have not really done much wildlife photography, but I'd like to try my hand at it at some point.

Facing East, we often get remarkable sunrises, particularly in the fall and winter when high clouds tend to form in the early morning.

Sacramento, CA and the surrounding region are known for their vibrant fall colors.

Portraits pose a special challenge for me. I find it very difficult to bring out the personality of the subject and not a mannequin.
